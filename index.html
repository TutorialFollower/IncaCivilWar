Huascar & Atahualpa

Huascar was chosen as the Sapa Inca next in line by his father Huayna Capac. He was born before 1527. Atahualpa was also a successor of the throne, and he would receive the northern part of the Inca empire in Quito. There are disputes as to who Atahualpa's mother could be but it is known that he was born in Cuzco.

The start of it all

In the early 1500s, Huayna Capac set out to make Quito a second capital, taking his son Atahualpa, one of his wives, and 50,000 soldiers. Along the way, he sent Atahualpa ahead with 20,000 soldiers, but a battle broke out in Yaguarcocha, forcing Atahualpa to flee. Huayna quickly intervened, defeated the opposition, and continued to Quito. There, he fell ill and declared Huascar as the heir to the empire while giving Quito to Atahualpa, a decision that would later ignite the Inca Civil War.

The last straw

After Huayna Capac’s death, tensions quickly started to rise between his sons, Huascar and Atahualpa. Huascar, inherited majority of the empire including Cuzco, made hasty decisions that strained the empire. Atahualpa who Inherited Quito saw himself as a separate nation from the Inca empire. Huascar did not like this as he considered himself to be the supreme ruler and tried to capture Atahualpa, despite Atahualpa's best efforts to negotiate. (See Picture of Huascar wielding an axe)

The last straw

After Huayna Capac’s death, tensions quickly started to rise between his sons, Huascar and Atahualpa. Huascar, inherited majority of the empire including Cuzco, made hasty decisions that strained the empire. Atahualpa who Inherited Quito saw himself as a separate nation from the Inca empire. Huascar did not like this as he considered himself to be the supreme ruler and tried to capture Atahualpa, despite Atahualpa's best efforts to negotiate. (See Picture of Huascar wielding an axe)

The onslaught

The war escalated when Huascar ordered his generals, Hango and Atoc, to go to Quito with 6,000 soldiers, wanting to capture Quito and Atahualpa. In response, Atahualpa gathered a much larger force of 60,000 soldiers led by his generals Chalcuchima, Quizquiz, and Rumiñahui. The two sides clashed in the Battle of Chilopampa in 1529, where Atahualpa defeated Huascar’s army.

Decades of Rich Inca History

This war was the downfall of the Inca empire as the Spanish used it to easily take over the empire when it was in a not so favorable position. Below is the full report on the Inca Civil War

In Juan De Betanzos’s book aptly titled, “The Narrative of the Incas” He details every piece of Inca history, even as far back as the Inca creation myth. The contributions that this book made are massive as it is very detailed. It is one of the very few first-hand accounts about the Inca civil war. In this book he said, Huyana Capac the emperor of the Incas was on a mission to Quito to make it the second capital of the Incan empire, along with his son Atahualpa, 50 thousand soldiers, and one of his wives. He left his other son Huascar in charge of the empire while he was gone. Huascar’s mother was the one wife of Huyana Capac going on the journey to Quito. On Huyana’s journey to Quito all the people that had approached him never left disappointed as Huyana was very noble and helped the poor in many ways. But one day on the journey, Huyana decided to rest in the province of Yaguarcocha and sent his son ahead with 20,000 soldiers. When the men of this district saw that these 20,000 soldiers were marching towards their land they were alarmed, causing the two sides to battle and, in the end, Atahualpa ended up fleeing since he was being defeated. When Huyana heard the news, he rushed towards them with all his men and defeated the opposing side. After this battle he comfortably made it to Quito where he had gotten sick and proclaimed Huascar would be the heir to the Inca empire and Atahualpa could have Quito, setting the stage for the Inca civil war (Betanzo, The Narratives of the Inca, 1557; University of Texas Press).

 Christopher Minster a professor at the University of San Fransico de Quito is a well-versed historian and is educated deeply within Latin American literature and history, in his post of the Inca civil war that is informative and helped contributed to show the actions of these two brothers before the war. Minster said that Huascar would prove to not be a very good ruler and made hasty decisions. This gave Atahualpa a hard time working with Huascar, so he tried many ways to contact Huascar and try to make the administration of the empire better, but Huascar had other plans. Huascar being in the capital had more soldiers and materials, but Atahualpa had the 3 largest and most powerful Inca generals, Chalcuchima, Quizquiz, Ruminahui so if war were to break out Huascar slightly had the upper hand... (Minster, 2024).

Titu Cusi Yupanqui was one of the last Inca rulers but virtually had no power because of the Spanish invasion of the South American continent. He wrote an account about the conquest about the Spanish in the Inca empire as well as briefly accounting the civil war which shed some light on the interactions between the two brothers during this time of tension. In his account he wrote, Atahualpa wanted to settle the rising tension through negotiation and peace, but Huascar rejected all of Atahualpa's proposals even managing to capture him for a brief amount of time (Yupanqui, An Inca account of the Conquest of Peru, 1570; Journal of Anthropological Research).

Cuzco, a book highly detailing the events that happened during the Colonization of the Americas, especially the Spanish conquest of the Inca. An author by the name of Si Sheppard decided to share an excerpt from the book that did not make it into the final edition of the book. This post by Dr. Sheppard contributed the details about the battles that unfolded during this war. In his post Dr. Sheppard shared what happened in the following sentences, it is still unknown as to which side started the conflict but according to Juan De Bezantos, the first war started when Huascar ordered his generals, Hango and Atoc to march to Quito with 6,000 men. When Atahualpa heard the news, he ordered his generals, Chalcuchima, QuizQuiz, and Ruminahui to get 60,000 soldiers ready. Atahualpa quickly advanced and this first battle resulted in a victory for Atahualpa. This was the battle of Chilopampa in 1529. The subsequent battles were all in favor of Atahualpa. While Atahualpa marched southward he committed massacres to instill fears in the people who had allied with Huascar. Finally, when Atahualpa reached Cuzco the battle of Quimpaipan took place. Atahualpa ambushed Huascar in an intricate trap leading to the end of the war in 1532 and later Huascar was killed in 1533 (Sheppard, 2021).  This war had a massive amount causalities but no exact number was ever recorded, so it estimated that more than 50 thousand people had died.

Finally, in a chronical written by Fransico Pizzaro a Spanish conquistador he details the Spanish invasion of the Inca empire and the execution of Atahualpa. In his chronical he also writes about the invasion of Peru after Atahualpa dies. This chronicle contributes a detailed explanation on the execution of Atahualpa. Pizzaro captured Atahualpa during what was supposed to be a negotiation. Atahualpa and his troops approached Pizzaro showing off their wealth. Friar Vincente approached Atahualpa with Christian artifacts, but Atahualpa dismissed them in an angry mood since he did not know what they were. This made the Spaniards mad, and they used this to justify an attack on Atahualpa and his troops who were unarmed at this time. The capture of Atahualpa marked the Spanish victory over the Inca and now only execution was waiting. Atahualpa tried to get his way out of execution by promising enormous rooms filled with gold and silver. Although he kept his word the Spaniards still decided to execute him. As a last-ditch effort, he converted to Christianity but that only changed his method of execution from being burnt to being strangled. At the end Pizzaro noted that Atahualpa was buried with much honor and he even acknowledged how composed he was even in captivity (Pizzaro, Fransico Pizzaro and the Conquest of the Inca, 1534; Mountjoy, Infobase publishing).

I think this topic should be more well known because it is the literal downfall of the Inca Empire. The civil war shows how the Spanish used a crucial time to invade and take over the Inca empire, waiting for the whole thing to blow over. Without knowing the details on how the Inca empire fell, we cannot just say the Inca empire fell because the Spanish invaded, as there is much more going on and the deceptions and tactics that were used must be brought to light.
